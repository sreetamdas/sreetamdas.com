# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

The primary audience is peers and collaborators: engineers, open-source contributors, newsletter readers, hiring-adjacent visitors, and curious people who want to understand Sreetam's work, taste, interests, and technical range. They usually arrive from search, GitHub, social links, shared posts, or direct referrals, then skim for credibility, personality, writing, projects, or ways to follow up.

## Product Purpose

This site is Sreetam Das's personal website, blog, and playground. It should make the person behind the work clear: a senior software tinkerer who writes, builds, over-engineers for fun, and cares about React, TypeScript, Elixir, Cloudflare, mechanical keyboards, games, and developer tooling. Success means visitors leave with a specific sense of craft and personality, can find writing and side projects easily, and feel invited to explore without the site turning into a portfolio template.

## Brand Personality

Curious, technical, playful. The voice should feel like a capable engineer showing their workshop: precise about implementation details, casual when the moment allows, and willing to hide a game or odd detail for visitors who poke around. Design work should preserve the site's sense of tinkering and avoid sanding everything into a neutral professional brochure.

## Anti-references

Avoid generic SaaS polish: repeated feature cards, vague startup copy, ornamental gradients, and anonymous launch-page structure. Avoid an overly restrained blog aesthetic that makes the site feel interchangeable or drains the playful personality. Avoid heavy agency-portfolio spectacle that competes with the writing, slows down reading, or makes every page feel like a showcase slide.

## Design Principles

- Keep the workshop visible: expose enough technical detail, oddity, and experimentation to make the site feel authored.
- Make reading easy first: typography, spacing, contrast, and navigation should support long-form content before decorative moves.
- Reward exploration: small hidden affordances and playful paths are part of the identity, but they should never block primary reading or navigation.
- Prefer specific artifacts over claims: show projects, posts, tools, stats, source links, and implementation details instead of generic personal-brand statements.
- Preserve approachable confidence: the site can be opinionated and precise without becoming loud, corporate, or self-serious.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target WCAG 2.2 AA for color contrast, keyboard navigation, focus visibility, semantic structure, and motion preferences. Dark and light schemes should both remain readable. Motion and playful interactions should respect `prefers-reduced-motion`, and hidden or experimental features must not trap keyboard users or screen reader users.
